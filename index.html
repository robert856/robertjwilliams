Voyage of the Dawn Treader

It was so much fun to design the set for The Voyage of the Dawn Treader, and I was even able to create some set choreography for the first time. We obtained the licensing from the C. S. Lewis Foundation in England for our stage adaptation. You have to get creative with limited time and a tight budget. We are an all-volunteer community theatre.

The Voyage of the Dawn Treader was such an ambitious play, with all the different sets that had to be created. Some scenes were elaborate, while others were abstract and minimalistic. In the book, C. S. Lewis describes in detail Lucy Pevensie entering and wandering through the rooms and doorways of the mysterious Magician's Mansion. Since there was no dialogue in that section, I had to convey it creatively on stage. I called it the "Dance of the Doorways." I've designed furniture for manufacturers as my profession and design sets as a volunteer, but discovering choreography as a new artistic medium for myself was like discovering a new treasure to be mined.

Pride and Prejudice

Jane Austen is such a great storyteller. Her characters and dialogue rank alongside those of Shakespeare, Dickens, and Chaucer. The beautiful settings and houses in Pride and Prejudice become almost characters themselves in her book, and that is exactly what I aimed to achieve with the set design. I wanted each house — Longbourn, Netherfield Park, Pemberley, and Rosings Park — to have its own distinct color palette and emotional feel. The biggest challenge was making seamless set changes within fifteen seconds without blackouts as we transitioned between the different homes. Several people mentioned they even "enjoyed watching the sets change."

Prince Caspian

C.S. Lewis's Prince Caspian is a grand adventure featuring the Pevensie children, and bringing it to the stage was an adventure in itself. Working with large moving set pieces in our small theatre, with very limited wing space, was challenging, but that is the fun of theatre. The ruins of the Treasure Room was such a large set piece that I was able to repurpose it later in the play as Aslan's How simply by adding a painting of Aslan in the doorway. The script and support materials were licensed from the Logos Theatre, and their resources and design suggestions were extremely helpful.

The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe

C.S. Lewis's The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe was the first play for which I designed a set. I organized volunteer work days and built the set myself. Of course, the wardrobe was the most fun to design and build. Because of the limited budget, I created Narnia with a more stylized diorama feel. Designing the stone table and the mechanism that would break apart was especially fun. I also made the sleigh do double duty: it was painted black on one side for the White Witch and red on the other for Father Christmas.
